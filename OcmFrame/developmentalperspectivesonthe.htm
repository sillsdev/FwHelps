Developmental perspectives on the OCM

In 1971, two articles were published in HRAFs quarterly journal, Behavior Science Notes (Volume 6, No. 3), reviewing the development and contemporary problems of the OCM. These articles by Clellan S. Ford and Frank W. Moore, the former President and Executive Director of HRAF, respectively, are well worth reading by anyone seriously interested in the utilization of the OCM or in the general issue of subject classification systems in the social/behavioral sciences.

Of particular interest here is the point emphasized by Moore that the OCM is sometimes regarded as a static product of the scholarship of the 1930s, whereas nothing could be further from the truth. "The OCM has changed and rechanged in literally thousands of ways since its inception." It is "constantly being revised and rethought" (p. 189).

In the fifty-one years that the HRAF research staff has been applying the OCM to the classification of descriptive information on cultures throughout the world, the OCM has been found to be both a highly flexible and adaptive system and an intricately integrated system. New concepts have been linked to existing subject categories, and categories have been modified and expanded to cover new ranges of information. Occasionally, and only after careful consideration of all possible ramifications that can be envisaged, new subject categories have been added to the OCM (see the preface for examples of categories added or modified for this version). Care must be taken not to change the system too radically so as not to lose continuity with the hundreds of thousands of text pages that have already been classified according to the OCM system in the HRAF Files.

Many of the above considerations are well-exemplified in HRAFs attempt to introduce a new group of subject categories in 1977-79. At that time, HRAF decided to build some Files on modern urban areas and to consider developing new types of Files on modern national states or sectors of such states. It was thought that a number of new categories would be needed to handle new kinds of urban data and large amounts of information on intermediate levels of administrative-political units between local communities and national governments. After much discussion, four new major category divisions were considered: Towns, Cities, Districts, and Provinces. Of course, each of these major divisions were subdivided into several three-digit categories. These new categories were to supersede the categories in division 630, where there is only one three-digit category for each of the four administrative levels. The first opportunity to test this new group of categories came with the development of the Okayama File (Outline of World Cultures code, AB43). The File unit is Okayama Prefecture within the modern state of Japan; it contains both rural and urban areas, and the data cover all administrative-political levels. The net result was that the use of these new categories was discontinued after completion of the Okayama File. The new categories proved to be too cumbersome and created more difficulties than they solved. 4
